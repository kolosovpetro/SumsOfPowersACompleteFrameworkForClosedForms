In this manuscript, we derive formulas for sums of powers by combining
centered Newton's interpolation formula for powers
\eqref{prop:newtons-interpolation-formula-for-powers}
with hockey-stick identities
\eqref{prop:generalized-hockey-stick-identity}
and
\eqref{lem:shifted-generalized-hockey-stick-identity}.

This approach can be generalized further. In particular, one may replace
classical finite difference operators with more general interpolation frameworks
based on abstract operators $D$ and generalized binomial expressions $\binom{f(n)}{k}$.
This allows us to go beyond linear difference operators such as $\Delta$, $\nabla$, or $\delta$.

Such non-linear difference operators arise naturally in the theory of dynamic equations on time scales,
developed by Bohner and Peterson in~\cite{Bohner2001DynamicEO}.
In this setting, the notion of discrete change is encoded by a function $g(h)$ rather than a linear increment.
A well-known example is Jackson’s $q$-derivative~\cite{jackson_1909}, defined by
\begin{align*}
  D_q f(x) = \frac{f(qx) - f(x)}{qx - x}.
\end{align*}

Motivated by this viewpoint, an abstract interpolation formula for powers may be written as
\begin{align*}
  n^m = \sum_{k} \binom{f(n)}{k}\, D(n^m, k),
\end{align*}
where $D(\cdot,k)$ represents a generalized difference functional.

Consequently, one obtains a family of formulas for sums of powers of the form
\begin{align*}
  \KnuthRFoldSum{1}{n}{m}
  = \sum_{k} D(n^m, k)\sum_{j \leq n} \binom{f(j)}{k}.
\end{align*}

Similarly, for multifold sums,
\begin{align*}
  \KnuthRFoldSum{r}{n}{m}
  = \sum_{k} D(n^m, k)\binom{f(n+r)}{k}.
\end{align*}

Many interpolation schemes can be expressed in terms of factorial-type bases.
In particular, rising and falling factorials are related to binomial coefficients via
\begin{align*}
  \frac{\fallingFactorial{x}{n}}{n!} &= \binom{x}{n}, \quad
  \frac{\risingFactorial{x}{n}}{n!} = \binom{x+n-1}{n}, \\
  \centralFactorial{x}{n} &= n \fallingFactorial{x+\frac{n}{2}-1}{n-1}.
\end{align*}

A notable example is Knuth’s formula for multifold sums of odd powers~\cite{knuth1993johann}:

\begin{proposition}[Multifold sums of odd powers]
  For non-negative integers $r,n,m$, we have
  \begin{align*}
    \KnuthRFoldSum{r}{n}{2m-1}
    &= \sum_{k=1}^{m} \binom{n+k-1+r}{2k-1+r}
    \frac{1}{2k}\,\delta^{2k} 0^{2m} \\
    &= \sum_{k=1}^{m} \binom{n+k-1+r}{2k-1+r}
    (2k-1)!\, T(2m, 2k),
  \end{align*}
  where $T(n,k)$ denotes the central factorial numbers of the second kind,
  see~\cite[Section 58]{steffensen1927interpolation, carlitz1963divided}.
\end{proposition}

This identity follows from Newton’s central difference formula evaluated at zero
(see~\cite{kolosov_2025_18098442}), together with the relation
\begin{align*}
  T(n,k)
  = \frac{1}{k!}\delta^k 0^n
  = \frac{1}{k!}\sum_{j=0}^{k} (-1)^j \binom{k}{j}
  \left(\frac{k}{2}-j\right)^n.
\end{align*}

More generally, central factorial numbers satisfy the polynomial identity
(see~\cite[Ch. 6.5, (24)]{riordan1968combinatorial})
\begin{lemma}[Riordan power identity]
  For integers $n,m \ge 0$,
  \begin{align*}
    n^m = \sum_{k=1}^{m} T(m,k)\,\centralFactorial{n}{k},
  \end{align*}
  where
  \begin{align*}
    \centralFactorial{n}{k}
    = n \prod_{j=0}^{k-1}\left(n+\frac{k}{2}-j\right),
    \quad \centralFactorial{n}{0}=1.
  \end{align*}
\end{lemma}

The values $T(2n,2k)$ correspond to OEIS sequence
\href{https://oeis.org/A008957}{A008957}~\cite{sloane2003line}.
